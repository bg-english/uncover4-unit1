📘 Used to & Would — Past Habits

The Big Question: Past Habits

Both used to and would can describe things we did regularly in the past — but they are not always interchangeable.

✅ USED TO — Two Jobs
  • Past habits (repeated actions) → I used to play football.
  • Past states (situations, feelings) → I used to live in Bogotá.

Formula: Subject + used to + base verb

✅ WOULD — One Job Only
  • Past habits (repeated, dynamic actions only) → I would go swimming every Sunday.
  • ❌ NEVER use WOULD with static/state verbs.

❌ I would know the answer.  ✅ I used to know the answer.

⚠️ The Key Rule to Remember
  • If the verb is DYNAMIC (action) + repeated → both work.
  • If the verb is STATIC (state/feeling) → only USED TO.
  • If it's a one-time past fact → only USED TO (or simple past).

Side-by-Side Examples

SentenceUsed to ✓Would ✓
I ___ play in the park daily.✅✅
She ___ love cartoons.✅❌ (state)
We ___ visit grandma on Sundays.✅✅
He ___ have a dog named Rex.✅❌ (state)
They ___ believe in Santa Claus.✅❌ (state)
I ___ eat at that restaurant.✅✅

Negative & Question Forms

Negative
  • I didn't use to like vegetables. (spoken)
  • I never used to wake up early. (common)
Question
  • Did you use to have a curfew?
  • Note: "use to" (no -d) in questions and negatives.
📗 Static vs Dynamic Verbs

What's the difference?

DYNAMIC verbs describe actions — things you actively DO. You can use them in continuous tenses.

STATIC verbs describe states — things that just ARE. They are NOT normally used in -ing forms.

Common Static Verbs (memorize these!)

knowbelievelove hatewantneed ownhave (possess)seem appearunderstandremember forgetbelongprefer meanconsistdepend containinclude

Common Dynamic Verbs

runplayeat writestudytalk cookdanceswim travelbuildjump think (actively)lookwork readlistenvisit

⚡ Tricky Verbs — Context Matters!

Some verbs can be BOTH depending on meaning:

VerbStatic meaningDynamic meaning
haveI have a car. (possess)I'm having lunch. (eating)
thinkI think it's late. (opinion)I'm thinking about you. (process)
seeI see the problem. (understand)I'm seeing a doctor. (meeting)
tasteIt tastes good. (quality)She's tasting the soup. (action)
